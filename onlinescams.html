Never Get Caught Again in Online Forex/ Crypto Scams! 

The trading and mentorship niche is very lucrative and worth millions of dollars.

This explains why so many self-proclaimed "retail professionals" offer courses and mentorship to trade stocks, options, forex, futures, and trading signals.
They are essentially marketers and not traders.

 Before jumping at the first opportunity and committing yourself, follow my checklist below to understand what to look for and the difference between a dodgy offering and a (qualified)legitimate one.

I assume you want a qualified professional trader to help you, right? Why bother with amateurs who are not qualified? It's not worth the time, money, and effort to talk to them.

Here is what you do when you arrive on the site’s home page. First of all, check the prefix to the website. If it starts with http, be wary because it’s not secure. If the web address starts with https, it’s a secure site. Never enter personal information when the address begins with http.

1. Check the "about us" section first. Who are they, what experience do they have, and are they qualified (FCA approved)? If not, stay away.
Fake sites seldom have good “about us” pages. Use your judgment here.

If there are no personal credentials, stay away.
Sometimes, they say they have institutional experience but never mention the institution's name. Google their names to see what comes up. Check if they are registered.

If they have photos of themselves, save the photo and go to a website called tineye dot com. Here, you can check where else the image shows up on the net. For example, I found the staff on one site that were all fake profiles.
If anything looks fishy - stay away.

2. Do a check-up on who owns the site.
Are the owner's details hidden or showing? Many fake sites conceal their names and addresses.
Go here to check- https://www.whois.com/
If you find the information says -redacted for privacy, it's not wrong. However, if you can't get any information about the responsible persons for the website, it’s a red flashing light!

3. Look up the company registration (generally mentioned in the footer of the page (links)
See who owns the company and if it’s registered with the SEC or on the scraped list. Remember, if it’s not a registered company, it will be hard to recover any funds or report the incident if you have had dealings with them.

- Unregistered Soliciting Entities: https://www.sec.gov/enforce/public-alerts
- Company filings- https://www.sec.gov/edgar/searchedgar/companysearch
- USA - https://www.finra.org/filing-reporting
- UK- https://www.fca.org.uk/firms/financial-services-register
- Australia/Asia - https://connectonline.asic.gov.au/

4. Read the website’s home page and check for typos and poor grammar. This is an obvious giveaway.
5. See if they have a LinkedIn profile. I found some are active on LinkedIn. Please make contact with them and see what response you get.
Many scam websites have newer site addresses. Most I found were registered in Iran, Turkey, Iceland, and Russia.

6. If they have a live chat on the home page, is the live chat an automated response?

7. Do annoying pop-ups show who has just joined or made thousands of dollars today?

8. Do they promise guaranteed returns? The promise of daily % returns, notice the amounts of money they want you to invest!

9. What terms and conditions of the site do they have?

10. Payments - Avoid payment options mentioning- direct wire transfers, money orders, or payment in crypto.
And non-refundable form of payment.

11. Only use secure payment methods with an address starting with https. NOT http.
More ads than content

12. Test their support emails and phone numbers

13. Check for reviews of the domain name

I hope you learned a lot here, and please stay away from a “Too good to be true offer.” If I missed something, let me know.
Thanks.

Lastly, Report fraud: https://www.sec.gov/tcr
